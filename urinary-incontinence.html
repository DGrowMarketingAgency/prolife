Urinary Incontinence

What is Urinary Incontinence?

Urinary incontinence is the loss of bladder control. It affects up to one in three women at some time in their life. The severity can range from occasional loss of urine when you cough or sneeze to needing to wear a pad daily to protect yourself or having a sudden and strong urge to urinate that makes it difficult to reach the toilet on time. Leaking urine can make your personal life and relationships very difficult, so it’s important to speak up and seek help.

How You May Feel

Depending on the type of urinary incontinence you have, the way you experience loss of urine will be different. The two main types of urinary incontinence are stress and urge incontinence.

Stress incontinence occurs when the ligament and muscle supports of the bladder outlet are not working properly. This means when you cough, strain, jump or lift, there isn’t enough strength to keep the bladder closed and you lose urine.

Urge incontinence may be the result of the bladder muscle contracting when it should be relaxed. This leads to a sudden and strong urge to pass urine, needing to rush to the toilet and potentially not making it in time.

In many cases, both types can occur at the same time — this is called mixed urinary incontinence.

See How We Can Help You

We will take a full history to determine the type of incontinence you are experiencing. We will also assess factors that may have caused or worsened your symptoms including medications, smoking, caffeine intake, constipation, obesity, coughing, heavy lifting, and other medical conditions.

Although symptoms may suggest which type of incontinence is present, further evaluation may be required — including a pelvic and vaginal examination, a bladder diary, infection tests, ultrasound, or specialized urinary tests called urodynamic studies or even a cystoscopy to look at the inside of your bladder.

Based on this information, we will discuss management options that best suit your situation. These can range from simple behavior changes, medications to improve your vaginal tissues or reduce bladder irritability, to surgical procedures.

A lot can be done to help you. The first step is understanding that accidentally losing urine is not normal — and to seek help.
